Why I built this

Every time I set up a Gibson Assembly, I'd open the same spreadsheet, retype my fragment lengths and concentrations, and work through the molar-ratio math to figure out how much of each piece to pipette. It worked — but it was slow, easy to fumble, and one mistyped cell could quietly throw off a whole reaction. When you're staring at a bench full of fragments before a cloning step, the last thing you want is arithmetic standing between you and the pipette.

So I built the version I actually wanted: a page where I type the numbers once and the volumes appear immediately, recalculating live as I go. It's a faithful port of the Barrick Lab's Gibson calculator spreadsheet — same trusted math, just instant, harder to get wrong, and open in a browser tab instead of buried in my files.

The whole point: the math should be invisible. You bring the fragments; the tool hands you a pipetting plan you can trust and act on right away.

How it works

  1. Enter your vector

    The first row is your backbone. Type its length in base pairs and its concentration in ng/µL — this is the reference everything else is scaled against.

  2. Add your inserts

    Add a row per insert with its length and concentration. Leave the fold-excess blank to use the NEB default, or type your own molar ratio.

  3. Set the reaction volume

    Adjust v_max, the volume available for fragments. The tool solves for how much of each fragment — and how much water — to add.

  4. Read off the volumes

    Each row shows the microliters to pipette, along with mass and picomole amounts, updating live as you edit.

  5. Export or save

    Copy the table, export a CSV, or save the whole setup as a named preset for next time.

The math, made honest

Gibson Assembly works best when fragments are combined at the right molar ratios, not equal volumes or equal masses. A short insert and a long vector at the same concentration hold very different numbers of molecules, so the calculator converts everything to picomoles and scales each fragment to its target fold excess against the vector.

Under the hood it reproduces the Barrick Lab spreadsheet exactly. For fragment n, the volume is v_n = (f_n · l_n / c_n) · (m_1 / l_1), where f is the fold excess, l the length, and c the concentration. The vector mass m_1 is the minimum of three caps — the volume you have, a picomole ceiling (0.5 pmol for 2–3 fragments, 1 pmol for 4+), and a 100 ng hard limit — so the reaction never over-concentrates. I didn't invent this math; I just made it instant and copied it faithfully, with the original spreadsheet kept in the repo for provenance.
