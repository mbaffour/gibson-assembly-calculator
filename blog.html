What it does

The molar-ratio math, done correctly, every time

Everything you need to go from fragment lengths and concentrations to a pipetting scheme you can trust — and nothing you don't.

⚡

Live recalculation as you type

Volumes, masses, and pmol amounts update on every keystroke. There is no "calculate" button to forget — the answer is always the current answer, and a cell gives a small visual cue the moment the reaction becomes solvable.

🧬

NEB-recommended fold excesses by default

The vector sits at 1×; inserts default to 5× if ≤ 200 bp and 2.5× otherwise, exactly as NEBuilder recommends. Every fold excess is overridable per row, and the fold actually used is printed back to you in its own column.

🎛️

Custom molar ratio

One optional input applies a custom molar fold excess to every insert that has no per-row override — useful when your protocol calls for a ratio other than the NEB defaults. Leave it blank and the standard defaults stand.

➕

Add and remove fragments freely

Start with a vector and one insert; add rows for as many fragments as your assembly needs. The first row is always the reference vector and is protected from deletion, so the math never loses its anchor.

🎚️

Editable vmax

The volume available for fragments defaults to 5 µL — leaving room for a 1.33× master mix — but you can set it to whatever your protocol uses. The water volume falls out automatically so your total always lands on target.

✖️

Master mix for N reactions

Enter a reaction count and a scaled per-component volume table appears, so you can pipette one batch instead of running the numbers N times. The export gains a matching ×N volume column. The single-reaction math is untouched.

🖨️

Printable protocol

One click opens a clean, self-contained page with your reaction parameters and volumes table — including the ×N master-mix column when it applies — and hands it to your browser's print or "Save as PDF" dialog for the bench.

💾

CSV export and clipboard copy

Export the full result table as a CSV, or copy it tab-separated straight into a lab notebook or spreadsheet. Roles, labels, lengths, concentrations, fold, volume, mass, and pmol amount all come along, plus the water row.

🗂️

Named presets

Save a reaction setup under a name and reload it later — stored in your browser via localStorage, so your go-to constructs are one click away. Presets carry the newer parameters too, and older saved presets still load cleanly.

✨

One-click worked example

Load a three-fragment example — a 7 kb vector and two inserts — to see a full reaction solved end to end. It's the fastest way to sanity-check the tool against a case you can reason about before trusting it with your own numbers.

📐

The three molar limits, shown

The vector mass m1 is the minimum of a volume limit, a molarity limit, and a hard 100 ng cap. The chosen pmax and the resulting m1 are surfaced in the parameters panel, so you can see which constraint is binding.

🔒

Runs entirely client-side

The whole thing is HTML, CSS, and one small JavaScript file. There is no backend, no analytics, and no network call after the page loads. Your fragment lengths and concentrations never leave the browser tab.

🔎

Accessible, screen-reader-friendly results

The results table is an aria-live region, so screen-reader users hear the updated volumes as they type rather than being left in silence. The interface is plain, labelled, and keyboard-navigable throughout.

Why this exists

The story behind the calculator

In this post

The predicament The actual problem What I built On building tools Built with Claude

Before every cloning reaction, there is a small ritual I never chose. Open the Gibson spreadsheet. Type in the length and concentration of the vector. Type in each insert. Squint at the molar-ratio column, check that the fold excesses are the ones I meant, and copy the resulting microlitres onto a scrap of paper next to the pipette. It takes a few minutes, it is dull, and it is exactly the kind of task where a tired brain makes a quiet mistake.

And a mistake here is expensive in the currency that matters least fairly: time. A slipped decimal, or a fold excess left at the wrong value, does not announce itself. You pipette a perfectly reasonable-looking reaction with slightly wrong ratios, incubate it, transform it, plate it, wait — and only two days later, staring at a plate that should have colonies and doesn't, do you start to wonder whether the arithmetic was ever right.

The math is not hard. That is precisely why it is dangerous — it is easy enough to feel beneath your attention, and important enough that getting it wrong quietly ruins an experiment.

So I did what I have started doing whenever a small, repeated task keeps costing me more than it should: I remembered that you can just build things.

The actual problem

To be clear, the spreadsheet is excellent. The one I use is Ben's Gibson Assembly calculator from the Barrick Lab's cloning protocol, and the method it encodes is sound — it is the reason my reactions work at all. The problem was never the math. The problem is the packaging. A spreadsheet is a wonderful thing to author a formula in and a slightly awkward thing to use under bench conditions, three tabs deep, when what you actually want is a single, instant, unambiguous answer to "how much of each do I pipette?"

The formula itself is compact. The volume of the n-th fragment is v_n = (f_n · l_n / c_n) · (m_1 / l_1), where the vector mass m_1 is the minimum of three constraints — a volume limit, a molarity limit set by p_max, and a hard 100 ng cap. Every one of those pieces is knowable, and every one of them is a place to make a copy-paste error at six in the evening. What I wanted was for the formula to be locked away where I couldn't fat-finger it, and for the inputs — the four numbers that actually change from reaction to reaction — to be the only things I ever touch.

The hidden cost was not really the minutes. It was the low, background friction of not trusting the number: re-checking it, doubting it, letting the reaction setup be a small source of unease instead of a solved problem. I wanted setting up a Gibson reaction to be a thirty-second job that I never had to think twice about.

What I built

The calculator is a single static page. You open it in the browser, type in each fragment's length and concentration, and the volumes appear as you type — no button to press, no server to talk to, nothing leaving your machine. The vector defaults to a 1× fold excess; inserts default to the NEB-recommended 5× if they are 200 bp or shorter and 2.5× otherwise. Every fold excess is overridable, and the tool prints back the fold it actually used, so there is never any ambiguity about what was assumed.

Underneath, it is a faithful port of the Barrick Lab spreadsheet math — the same formula, the same three limits on the vector mass, the same conversion constant. I did not want to improve the method; I wanted to reproduce it exactly and then wrap it in something quicker to use. On top of that faithful core, I added the things I kept wishing the spreadsheet had. A custom molar ratio for protocols that deviate from the NEB defaults. A master-mix multiplier, so setting up a plate of N reactions is one number, not N passes through the arithmetic. A printable protocol you can save as a PDF and take to the bench. CSV export and clipboard copy for the lab notebook. Named presets for the constructs I build over and over. And, because a tool nobody can use is not really a tool, the results are announced to screen readers, and the whole thing ships under an MIT license.

None of these are clever. That is the point. The value is not in any single feature; it is in the sum being instant, correct, and reproducible, so that the reaction setup stops being a place where things go wrong.

On building tools as a scientist

I am a PhD candidate studying phage biology at Texas A&M, not a software engineer, and there is a version of scientific training that would call an afternoon spent building a calculator a distraction from the real work. There is real wisdom in that worry — the bench does not run itself, and infrastructure can become a very comfortable place to hide from an experiment.

But I have come to believe the opposite is more often true for the small stuff. Rebuilding this calculator forced me to actually understand the formula I had been trusting for years — which of the three limits usually binds, why p_max changes with fragment count, what the 100 ng cap is really protecting against. You do not get that from using a spreadsheet; you get it from having to make one behave.

Building the tool taught me the math I thought I already knew. Using it just saves me time.

I am not claiming this is a large or important piece of software. It is a small one that does one thing correctly. But it runs, it has already saved me more time than it took to build, and it turned a recurring source of quiet error into a solved problem. That seems like the right bar for whether something was worth doing.

A note on how it was built

This calculator was built collaboratively with Claude (Anthropic's AI). The idea, the frustration that motivated it, the decision to port the Barrick Lab math faithfully rather than reinvent it, and the calls about which features actually earned their place — those are mine. Claude was the implementation partner: writing and debugging the calculation engine, the export logic, the printable protocol, and the accessibility wiring. Working this way let me turn a specific bench frustration into a working, correct tool far faster than I could have alone, and I think it is worth being transparent about that.

Getting started

Open it and set up a reaction

The interface should be self-explanatory. If it is not, that is a bug — tell me.

01

Open it in your browser

Click the link above. No download, no account, no sign-up. It's a static page and works entirely offline once loaded — nothing you type ever leaves the tab.

02

Enter your vector first

The first row is the reference vector. Type its length in bp and concentration in ng/µL. If your assembly has no true vector, use the largest fragment as row one.

03

Add your inserts

Click Add a fragment for each insert and fill in length and concentration. Fold excesses default to the NEB recommendation, but you can override any of them per row.

04

Tune the parameters

Adjust v_max if your protocol differs, set a custom molar ratio if you need one, and enter a reaction count to see the scaled master-mix table for a batch.

05

Read off the volumes

The µL to pipette for each fragment — and for water — appear live as you type. The vector mass and the binding molar limit are shown in the parameters panel.

06

Export, print, or save a preset

Copy the table to your clipboard, export a CSV, print a formatted protocol, or save the whole setup as a named preset in your browser for next time.
